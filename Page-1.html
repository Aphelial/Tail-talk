TAIL TALK

Shih Tzu:

The Shih Tzu is a charming, toy-sized dog breed with a rich history and delightful personality. Known for their affectionate nature and distinctive appearance, they are a popular choice for families and individuals alike.

History:

The Shih Tzu originated in Tibet or China, with its ancestors believed to have been bred as companion dogs for Chinese royalty, particularly during the Ming and Qing dynasties.

The name Shih Tzu translate to lion dog in mandarin referencing to its resemblance to traditional lion statues.




FUN FACTS

Did You know that Shih Tzu has the nickname "chrysanthemum dog" due to the way their hair grows around their face. this nickname is often used to describe the unique and beautiful way their fur grows in a circular pattern, resembling the petals of a chrysanthemum flower.

Physical Characteristics:
The Shih Tzu is a small, sturdy dog, standing 9-11 inches tall and weighing 9-16 pounds. Its luxurious double coat comes in colors like gold, white, black, silver, and brindle. The breed has large, round eyes, a short muzzle, and a slight underbite, giving it a charming look. Its tail curls over its back, and its long, coated ears add to its regal appearance.

Personality and Behavior:
Shih Tzus are affectionate, loyal, and social, making them great companions for families, singles, and seniors. They enjoy playtime but prefer lounging in between. Despite their small size, they are alert and make good watchdogs. They get along well with children, strangers, and other pets, especially if socialized early.




Health Care:
Shih Tzus, like all breeds, are prone to certain health issues due to their physical traits and genetics. With proper care, they can live 10 to 16 years.
Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS): A genetic condition in Shih Tzus and other flat-faced breeds like Bulldogs and Pugs, causing breathing difficulties due to short snouts.
Eye Health Concerns: Shih Tzus’ large, protruding eyes make them prone to:Corneal ulcers
Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca
Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA)

Proactive Care: Regular vet visits, grooming, proper diet, and exercise improve their quality of life. Despite extra care needs, their companionship is priceless.
